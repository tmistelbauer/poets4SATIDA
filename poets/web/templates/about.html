    About

    The most widely used global soil moisture dataset for research purposes was developed within the Climate Change Initiative (CCI) of the European Space Agency (ESA) (ESA CCI SM) [1], [2]. It is composed of observations from nine different satellite missions and aims to exploit the individual strengths of active (radar) and passive (radiometer) sensors, thereby providing surface soil moisture estimates at a spatial resolution of 0.25 degrees. Currently, the ESA CCI SM dataset is available on a global scale, covering a period from 1978 to 2013. Operational applications require daily soil moisture updates. However, the ESA CCI SM dataset is only updated irregularly (roughly once a year). As a consequence, the product that we present here is based on the adaptation of the ESA CCI processing chain for daily updates via satellite-derived near real-time (NRT) soil moisture observations. In the current set-up we include NRT observations from the Advanced SCATterometer (ASCAT) on-board the MetOp satellites and the Advanced Microwave Scanning Radiometer 2 (AMSR-2) on-board the GCOM-W satellite. The dataset is provided as decadal (10-day) surface soil moisture averages at a spatial resolution of 0.25 degrees (roughly 28 kilometers at the equator). A corresponding publication is currently in preparation.

    Background

    Soil moisture, the water in the soils’ pore space, is one of very few environmental variables that directly link atmospheric processes to land surface conditions [3], [4]. Satellite instruments capable of retrieving information about soil moisture have been available since the late 1970s. The measurement principle of space-based microwave sensors exploits the dielectric properties of water, which have a direct impact on the soil’s reflectance of microwaves in case of active sensors (radars) or the emissivity of the soil surface in case of passive sensors (radiometers). One big advantage of microwave remote sensing is the opportunity to work independently from weather (e. g. cloud cover) or sunlight. Limitations include issues related to the retrieval of soil moisture over complex topography or dense vegetation.

    Despite the existence of several individual space-borne soil moisture products, a harmonized long-term dataset was missing until the Climate Change Initiative (CCI) of the European Space Agency (ESA) released the first multi-sensor soil moisture product [1], [2], [5]. The ESA CCI soil moisture dataset (ESA CCI SM) relies on the merging of different active (radar) and passive (radiometer) microwave instrument measurements to a single consistent product. Since the release of the first version in 2012 the dataset was used to identify global trends in soil moisture and vegetation [6]–[8] and to improve the understanding of land-atmosphere coupling [9], [10]. The latest release of this product (CCI SM v02.1) covers a period from 1978 to 2013.
